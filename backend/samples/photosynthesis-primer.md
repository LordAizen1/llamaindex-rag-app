# A Short Primer on Photosynthesis

## What Photosynthesis Is

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria
convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. The overall balanced
equation is: 6 CO2 + 6 H2O + light energy -> C6H12O6 + 6 O2. It is the primary
route by which energy from sunlight enters the biosphere.

## Where It Happens

In plants, photosynthesis takes place in organelles called chloroplasts, which
are most abundant in the mesophyll cells of leaves. Chloroplasts contain a green
pigment called chlorophyll, which absorbs light most strongly in the blue
(around 430 nm) and red (around 660 nm) parts of the spectrum and reflects green.

## The Light-Dependent Reactions

The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes. Light excites
electrons in chlorophyll at Photosystem II and Photosystem I. Water is split in a
process called photolysis, releasing oxygen as a byproduct. These reactions
produce ATP and NADPH, which carry energy to the next stage.

## The Calvin Cycle

The Calvin cycle, also called the light-independent reactions, occurs in the
stroma of the chloroplast. It uses ATP and NADPH from the light reactions to fix
carbon dioxide into glucose. The key enzyme is RuBisCO, which is considered the
most abundant protein on Earth. The cycle does not directly require light but
depends on the products of the light reactions.

## Factors Affecting the Rate

The rate of photosynthesis is influenced by light intensity, carbon dioxide
concentration, and temperature. Each can be a limiting factor: increasing light
raises the rate only until another factor becomes limiting. Most plants slow
photosynthesis sharply above about 40 degrees Celsius because enzymes denature.

## C3, C4, and CAM Plants

Most plants use the C3 pathway. C4 plants such as maize and sugarcane concentrate
carbon dioxide to reduce photorespiration and are more efficient in hot, sunny
climates. CAM plants such as cacti open their stomata at night to conserve water.
